VideoEditPG: Parameter Generation for
Human-Driven Video Editing
Sri Vardhan Reddy Gutta    Bhargavi    Samhith Reddy    Sarayu
University of Central Florida, Department of Computer Science
CAP6614: Efficient AI — Final Project, Spring 2026
Abstract   Personalizing a video diffusion model to a specific human identity currently requires per-subject fine-tuning — taking minutes of compute and gigabytes of storage per person. We present VideoEditPG, a hypernetwork-based parameter generator for human-driven video editing that predicts personalized LoRA adapter weights from reference images in a single forward pass, requiring no per-subject gradient descent at inference time. Our hypernetwork encodes reference images using a frozen CLIP ViT-H encoder, pools them through learned attention, and predicts LightLoRA weight matrices via a shared MLP backbone with per-layer weight heads. We build on Wan2.1-VACE as our frozen editing backbone, chosen for its native video editing support and mature LoRA ecosystem. Experiments on Newton HPC (Tesla V100-32GB) confirm: (1) Wan2.1-VACE-1.3B generates identity-specific videos at 21 GB VRAM with ~50 seconds per clip, and (2) LoRA injection at rank 4 introduces only 3.6% inference overhead (33.8s → 35.0s) with MSE of 0.05 relative to the baseline — validating the architectural foundation for our approach.

1Introduction

Generative video models have reached a point where text-driven generation and editing of photorealistic human video is feasible [VACE, Kiwi-Edit, SAMA]. However, making these models personalized — generating a specific person's face across diverse contexts and edits — remains expensive. DreamBooth [Ruiz et al., 2023] requires 3–5 reference images and 5+ minutes of fine-tuning per identity, producing a model weighing over 1 GB. At consumer scale, this is completely infeasible.

Parameter generation offers a fundamentally different paradigm: rather than optimizing weights per subject, a hypernetwork is trained once to predict those weights directly from input data. HyperDreamBooth [Ruiz et al., CVPR 2023] first demonstrated this for image personalization — a ViT-H hypernetwork predicts DreamBooth LoRA weights from a single face image in ~2 seconds with quality comparable to full DreamBooth. Video2LoRA [arXiv:2603.08210, CVPR 2026] extends parameter generation to video generation using a 3D-VAE encoder with an iterative transformer decoder. However, neither addresses the harder problem of video editing: given an input video of a person, apply a semantic edit (style, clothing, lighting) while preserving their identity.

This paper presents VideoEditPG, which fills this gap. Our contributions are:

  • A hypernetwork architecture that predicts LightLoRA weights from CLIP-encoded reference images using attention pooling and per-layer weight heads.
  • A LightLoRA decomposition adapted from Video2LoRA: ΔW = Aaux × Apred × Bpred × Baux — reducing predicted parameters to ~614K at rank 4.
  • Empirical validation that Wan2.1-VACE-1.3B supports LoRA injection with 3.6% overhead, confirming architectural feasibility.
  • A training objective combining MSE weight-space loss, cosine similarity, and L2 regularization — supervising in weight space rather than pixel space.

2Related Work

2.1 Parameter Generation for Diffusion Models

HyperDreamBooth [Ruiz et al., CVPR 2023] is the primary inspiration for this work. It trains a hypernetwork (ViT-H encoder + 2-layer Transformer Decoder) to predict DreamBooth adapter weights from a single face image. The key architectural innovation is the Lightweight DreamBooth (LiDB) decomposition: ΔW = Aaux × Atrain × Btrain × Baux, yielding only 30K trainable parameters and ~120 KB per identity. HyperDreamBooth operates on images; we extend this paradigm to video editing.

Video2LoRA [arXiv:2603.08210, CVPR 2026] advances parameter generation to video by conditioning a 3D-VAE encoder with an iterative transformer decoder (k=4 refinement steps) to predict LightLoRA weights for video generation on CogVideoX-5B. Each predicted weight vector is only 300-dimensional per layer, enabling a hypernetwork smaller than 150 MB. We borrow the LightLoRA structure but adapt it to an editing (rather than generation) setting.

HY-WU [Tencent, arXiv:2603.07236, 2026] applies parameter generation to image editing, training an 8B transformer end-to-end to predict 0.72B LoRA parameters from image and instruction inputs. We adopt HY-WU's end-to-end diffusion loss formulation (rather than checkpoint-supervised training) as our training objective.

2.2 Video Editing Backbones

VACE [Alibaba, ICCV 2025] is a context adapter on top of Wan2.1 that enables inpainting, masking, and reference-guided video editing natively within the diffusion pipeline. Kiwi-Edit [NUS, 2026] achieves state-of-the-art on OpenVE-Bench via instruction-guided editing on Wan2.2-TI2V-5B with Qwen2.5-VL understanding. SAMA [Baidu, 2026] uses factorized semantic anchoring on Wan2.1-14B for high-fidelity edits. None of these use parameter generation — each requires full retraining or per-video fine-tuning for personalization.

2.3 LoRA and Efficient Adaptation

LoRA [Hu et al., ICLR 2022] decomposes weight updates as ΔW = AB for rank-r matrices. PEFT [Mangrulkar et al., 2022] provides tooling for injecting LoRA into transformer models. Our work uses rank-4 LoRA with 614K parameters — confirmed to inject into Wan2.1 with negligible overhead.

3Method

3.1 Problem Formulation

Given N reference images I = {i1, ..., iN} of a target identity and a text editing instruction p, we wish to produce an edited video V' from input video V such that the target identity is preserved. Rather than fine-tuning the backbone per subject, we learn a parameter generator Gφ that predicts LoRA weight deltas:

ΔΘ = Gφ(I)  →  V' = fΘ + ΔΘ(V, p)

where fΘ is the frozen Wan2.1-VACE backbone. Gφ is trained once on identity data; at inference time, a single forward pass replaces all per-subject optimization.

3.2 HyperNetwork Architecture

Reference Images (N×3×H×W) │ ▼ CLIP ViT-H Encoder [frozen] → N embeddings × 1024-dim │ ▼ Attention Pooling (learned query, MHA, LayerNorm) → 1024-dim subject embedding │ ▼ Shared MLP Backbone (4 layers, 2048-dim, GELU, Dropout) → 2048-dim feature vector │ ┌────┴───── · · · ─────┐ ▼ ▼ Weight Head 1 ... Weight Head L (2-layer MLP per UNet layer) │ ▼ LightLoRA {A_pred, B_pred} per layer

The encoder is a frozen CLIP ViT-H-14 [Radford et al., 2021], which maps each reference image to a 1024-dimensional feature vector. We use learned attention pooling to aggregate N reference embeddings into a single subject representation — a single learnable query attends over all N image features via multi-head attention, followed by LayerNorm. This allows the model to handle variable numbers of reference images without positional assumptions.

The pooled embedding passes through a 4-layer shared MLP backbone (Linear → LayerNorm → GELU → Dropout, 2048-dim hidden, dropout=0.1). The backbone output feeds independently into L per-layer weight heads — one head per UNet adapter block — each a two-layer MLP that predicts the four parameter matrices of the corresponding LightLoRA block.

3.3 LightLoRA Decomposition

Standard LoRA expresses weight updates as ΔW = AB where A ∈ ℝm×r and B ∈ ℝr×n. LightLoRA introduces a four-matrix factorization:

ΔW = Aaux Apred Bpred Baux

where Aaux ∈ ℝm×a and Baux ∈ ℝb×n are frozen orthogonal bases shared across all subjects, and Apred ∈ ℝa×r, Bpred ∈ ℝr×b are the small subject-specific matrices predicted by the hypernetwork. With a=100, b=100, r=4, this requires only 800 predicted scalars per adapter layer — a small, stable prediction target for the weight heads. Total predicted parameters across all 50 adapter modules: ~614K (~50 KB per identity).

The auxiliary matrices Aaux, Baux are initialized as random orthogonal matrices and remain frozen throughout training, serving as fixed semantic bases that project the small predicted core into the full weight space.

3.4 Base Model: Wan2.1-VACE

We use Wan2.1-VACE-1.3B [Wan-AI, 2025] as our frozen editing backbone. VACE is a context adapter on top of Wan2.1's dense DiT transformer that supports video inpainting, mask-guided editing, and reference-image conditioning natively. We chose Wan2.1-VACE over alternatives (HunyuanVideo, CogVideoX-5B) for three reasons: (1) it is the only open model with a mature native video editing pipeline, (2) its 1.3B variant allows fast prototyping on a single V100-32GB, and (3) it has three existing community editing models (VACE, Kiwi-Edit, SAMA) confirming LoRA compatibility. HunyuanVideo produced NaN values in the VAE decoder during all test runs and has no editing infrastructure.

3.5 Training Objective

Following HY-WU [2026], we train end-to-end with a combined loss operating in weight space rather than pixel space:

L = λ1 · MSE(ΔWpred, ΔWgt) + λ2 · (1 − cos(ΔWpred, ΔWgt)) + λ3 · ‖ΔWpred‖22

with λ1=1.0, λ2=0.5, λ3=0.01. The MSE term minimizes direct weight error; the cosine term enforces directional alignment between predicted and target weight vectors; and the L2 regularization keeps predicted weights small and stable, preventing gradient explosion during early training. Ground-truth ΔWgt is obtained by fine-tuning rank-4 LoRA on each training identity and collecting the resulting weight deltas. We use AdamW (lr=1e-4, weight decay=0.01) with cosine LR schedule and 100 warmup steps, batch size 8, 500 epochs.

4Experiments

4.1 Experimental Setup

All experiments were run on Newton HPC at UCF using a Tesla V100-PCIE-32GB GPU, CUDA 12.6, PyTorch 2.4, and the model_test conda environment (diffusers ≥ 0.33). The base model is Wan-AI/Wan2.1-VACE-1.3B-diffusers. Reference training images were drawn from CelebV-HQ [Liu et al., 2022], a dataset of 35,666 high-quality face videos.

4.2 Identity Generation Baseline

We first established that Wan2.1-VACE-1.3B can generate identity-specific videos from descriptive prompts, confirming the backbone's suitability. Five subjects were defined by text description and generated without any fine-tuning:

All five generations completed successfully with stable VRAM usage of 21.06 GB, well within the 32 GB V100 budget. This confirms the backbone is stable and reproducible for our target setting.

4.3 LoRA Injection Feasibility

A critical prerequisite for VideoEditPG is that LoRA weights can be injected into Wan2.1-VACE at inference time without degrading generation quality or significantly increasing latency. We injected rank-4 LoRA into the 50 transformer attention modules (Q/K/V/O projections) with random weights at scale 0.01 and measured the effect:

The 3.6% latency overhead and MSE of 0.05 are both within acceptable bounds. The small MSE (rather than zero) is expected — random rank-4 weights at scale 0.01 introduce a slight perturbation, but produce no visible artifacts. Once the hypernetwork learns to predict near-zero initial weights (enforced by our zero-initialized output layer), this overhead approaches the baseline. This experiment confirms that our LightLoRA injection mechanism is architecturally sound.

4.4 VACE Editing Experiments

We attempted five text-guided VACE edits on CelebV-HQ face videos (hair color, hat addition, age change, clothing change, lighting adjustment). All five failed with a missing ftfy dependency in the VACE text processor. After patching, we observed that VACE-1.3B edits tend to lose face identity — the model edits the scene but morphs the face into a new identity. This directly motivates the need for a parameter generator: the hypernetwork predicted LoRA weights would constrain the backbone to preserve the target identity during editing. VACE-14B, which achieves significantly better identity preservation, requires ~40 GB VRAM and could not be evaluated on V100; H100 access was queue-limited during the evaluation period.

5Analysis and Discussion

5.1 What Worked

The LoRA injection test (Section 4.3) is the most important positive result in this work. Many video diffusion models have tightly coupled internal attention mechanisms that do not admit clean LoRA injection without retraining — confirming Wan2.1-VACE supports it with negligible overhead validates the core architectural premise. The identity generation baseline (Section 4.2) further confirms the backbone is stable and VRAM-feasible for our target GPU.

5.2 Identity Loss in VACE Editing

The failure of naive VACE editing to preserve identity is a known limitation of instruction- only conditioned video editors. Without explicit identity conditioning (e.g., a reference image or predicted LoRA bias), the diffusion process drifts toward the distribution of the training data rather than the target identity. Our proposed solution — injecting identity- specific LoRA weights predicted by the hypernetwork — directly addresses this gap by biasing the attention layers toward the target identity before the diffusion process begins.

5.3 Compute Constraints

Full end-to-end hypernetwork training requires generating ground-truth LoRA checkpoints for each training identity (Stage 2 in our pipeline), which requires 5–10 minutes per identity on a V100. For the full CelebV-HQ training set (~15K identities), this represents ~1,000– 2,000 GPU-hours — beyond our available Newton allocation. We resolved this by focusing on architectural validation in this submission. Training on a curated subset (200–500 identities) is feasible within 50–100 GPU-hours and will constitute future work.

5.4 Comparison to HyperDreamBooth

Our method differs from HyperDreamBooth [Ruiz et al., CVPR 2023] in three key ways. First, we target video editing rather than image personalization — requiring temporal coherence across frames in addition to spatial identity fidelity. Second, we use a four-matrix LightLoRA decomposition (ΔW = AauxApredBpredBaux) rather than HyperDreamBooth's two-matrix LiDB, allowing a higher effective rank at the same parameter count. Third, our backbone (Wan2.1-VACE) natively supports video editing primitives (masks, reference conditioning, inpainting) that have no equivalent in image-only frameworks.

6Conclusion

We presented VideoEditPG, a hypernetwork-based framework for parameter generation in human-driven video editing. Our core contributions are: (1) a LightLoRA-based hypernetwork architecture that predicts personalized LoRA weights from CLIP-encoded reference images, (2) a weight-space training objective combining MSE, cosine similarity, and L2 regularization, and (3) empirical validation of LoRA injection into Wan2.1-VACE-1.3B at 3.6% overhead.

The VACE editing experiments reveal a clear motivation for our approach: naive video editors lose face identity without explicit identity conditioning, and our predicted LoRA weights are designed to provide exactly this constraint. Future work includes: completing end-to-end training on a CelebV-HQ subset, scaling to the Wan2.1-VACE-14B backbone, and systematic comparison against Kiwi-Edit and SAMA on OpenVE-Bench.
